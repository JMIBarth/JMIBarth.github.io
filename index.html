Research interests

Biodiversity – all life on Earth – provides essential resources like food, shelter, and clean water. However, human population growth and unsustainable practices are disrupting ecosystems and accelerating species loss. This makes it urgent to understand how to protect biodiversity, not only by preserving habitats but also by studying how species cope with change. At the core of biodiversity is genetic diversity, which contains the information organisms need to develop, function, and adapt. Populations with higher genetic diversity are more likely to survive rapid environmental changes. Protecting this genetic variation is therefore a key goal in conservation. Yet, the processes shaping genetic diversity are still not fully understood and incorporating genetic diversity into conservation strategies is still a challenge.

Research projects

Over the past decade, I have used genomic approaches to uncover the evolutionary drivers of biodiversity in non-model organisms, with a strong focus on translating these insights into conservation practice. In particular, I have investigated three key sources of genetic diversity: de novo mutations, gene flow and hybridization, and structural variation.

  • Gene flow and hybridization

  • Genomic structural variation

  • Applied genomics in biodiversity management

Populations and speciation genomics for the future generation

Hands-on bioinformatics education is at the core of the Workshop on Population and Speciation Genomics. This intensive training program, led by a collaborative team of experts in evolutionary genomics, is designed to promote bioinformatics education and foster collaboration by bringing together researchers and professionals. In doing so, the workshop helps bridge communication gaps between science and practice, supporting the integration of genomics into conservation strategies.
